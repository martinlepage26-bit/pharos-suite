# Qualitative

Use this skill to turn broad qualitative-methods requests into a clear recommendation, comparison, or analysis plan.

## Start With the Material

Before choosing a method, identify what kind of material is actually available or feasible to collect.

- Interviews, focus groups, or open-ended responses usually support thematic, phenomenological, narrative, or category-building analysis.
- Fieldnotes, shadowing notes, and observed routines usually support ethnography or participant-observation-based analysis.
- Policy texts, media, speeches, and institutional documents usually support discourse analysis or CDA.
- Images, film, maps, and visual artifacts usually support visual or spatial analysis.
- Digital traces, forum threads, platform interactions, and online communities usually support digital ethnography or discourse work.
- Diaries, memoir, reflective journals, and self-writing usually support autoethnography, writing-mode analysis, or narrative inquiry.
- Life stories plus archives, witness accounts, or records usually support life-history or ethnosociological analysis.

If the user names both a question and a material, let the material constrain the shortlist.

## Then Clarify the Research Goal

Identify four things before choosing a method:

1. The main question: patterns, lived experience, process, language, culture, identity, power, or theory-building.
2. The unit of analysis: person, story, interaction, group, organization, space, image, platform, or document.
3. The data source: interviews, fieldnotes, archives, visuals, digital traces, or self-reflection.
4. The expected output: themes, categories, narrative arcs, typology, process model, or critical interpretation.

If the user is vague, make the smallest reasonable assumption and state it.

## Match Question to Method

- Use thematic analysis for pattern-finding across interviews, open-ended responses, or mixed qualitative datasets.
- Use category-based or grounded-theory-like analysis when the goal is to build concepts, dimensions, or relationships inductively.
- Use phenomenology when the goal is the lived experience or essential structure of a phenomenon from the participant perspective.
- Use narrative inquiry or life-history approaches when time, sequence, turning points, or identity construction matter.
- Use ethnography or participant observation when the question centers on practices, norms, rituals, or culture in context.
- Use discourse analysis or critical discourse analysis when meaning, ideology, framing, or power is carried through language.
- Use ethnomethodology when the goal is to study how people produce social order in interaction.
- Use autoethnography or writing-mode analysis when the researcher's situated experience is part of the analytic material.
- Use visual, sensory, spatial, or digital ethnography when images, embodied environments, place, or online life are central.

When several methods fit, compare them directly instead of listing everything. Prefer methods that are legible from the material already in hand.

## Build the Design

For any recommendation, specify:

1. Why the method fits the question better than the closest alternatives.
2. What data to collect.
3. How to sample.
4. What the core analytic moves are.
5. What the main risks or validity threats are.

Use purposive sampling by default. Switch to theoretical sampling when the design is category-building. Mention saturation when the user asks about sample sufficiency; for life-history or ethnosociological work, note that saturation is structural rather than statistical.

## Handle Core Qualitative Concepts

- Explain `emic` as the participant or insider lens.
- Explain `etic` as the analytic or outsider lens.
- Treat reflexivity and positionality as required, not optional, whenever the researcher is part of the field or interpretation.
- Surface ethics early for participant observation, online fieldwork, vulnerable communities, sexuality, health, disability, criminalization, or archived personal materials.
- Use intersectionality when race, gender, class, coloniality, disability, sexuality, migration, or environment shape the phenomenon.

## Shape the Response

Default response structure:

1. Best-fit method or shortlist.
2. Why it fits this material and question.
3. Suggested data and sampling plan.
4. Core analysis steps.
5. Key cautions, including ethics and reflexivity.

Avoid encyclopedic method dumps unless the user explicitly asks for a broad survey. Group nearby methods and highlight contrasts.
