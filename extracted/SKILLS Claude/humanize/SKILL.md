# Humanize

Turn governing rules into behavior-aware guidance without losing clarity, accountability, or required force.

## Start

- Identify the task type first:
  - rewrite a rule or policy
  - diagnose why a rule is failing
  - adapt a rule for a culture, audience, or power structure
  - build a checklist, framework, or leadership note
- Preserve hard constraints before changing tone:
  - law
  - physical safety
  - deadlines
  - thresholds
  - audit trails
  - mandatory controls
- State assumptions when the audience, jurisdiction, or enforcement stakes are unclear.

## Default Stance

- Design for willing people first; reserve escalation for willful misconduct.
- Apply Rule Five: if many people break a rule, treat it as a rule-design problem until evidence suggests otherwise.
- Apply Rule Six: "Just because you can, doesn't mean you should."
- Prefer compliance by design: define the desired behavior or outcome first, then write the rule to support it.
- Judge controls by real-world performance, not only by how complete they look on paper.
- Keep required obligations explicit. Human-centered language should reduce friction, not hide the requirement.

## Rewrite Workflow

1. Extract the non-negotiables.
2. Identify who must act, what they must do, when they must do it, and why it matters.
3. Rewrite in plain language with active voice and verb-led sentences.
4. Add brief context so the rule feels purposeful, not arbitrary.
5. Keep enforcement language proportionate and clear.

Read [redraft.md](/home/cerebrhoe/.codex/skills/humanize/references/redraft.md) when actively rewriting text.

## Diagnostic Workflow

1. Describe the behavior that is not happening.
2. Check for capability, opportunity, motivation, attention, and social-environment causes before blaming intent.
3. Separate problems caused by design, tooling, training, incentives, or culture from truly malicious behavior.
4. Recommend redesign, enablement, or enforcement based on the cause.

Read [diagnose.md](/home/cerebrhoe/.codex/skills/humanize/references/diagnose.md) when evaluating a failing rule or control.

## Cultural Calibration

- Keep the obligation level stable while tuning tone, directness, pronouns, and examples.
- Use firmer wording when "should" would read like an optional suggestion.
- Avoid over-softening a requirement into something people can ignore.
- Avoid over-formalizing a rule in ways that trigger distrust, distance, or reactance.

Read [culture.md](/home/cerebrhoe/.codex/skills/humanize/references/culture.md) when the audience spans countries, institutions, or power norms.

## Output Patterns

- For rewrites, provide:
  - a short diagnosis of what is wrong with the original
  - the revised rule
  - a brief note explaining what changed
- For diagnostics, provide:
  - the observed failure
  - likely behavioral causes
  - the redesign or governance response
- For high-stakes rules, provide two layers when useful:
  - a strict legal or policy baseline
  - a human-facing operational version

## Guardrails

- Do not soften statutory, safety-critical, or legally fixed requirements into optional language.
- Do not confuse empathy with vagueness.
- Do not replace concrete obligations with values-only prose.
- When enforceability matters, preserve mandatory verbs such as `must` or `need to` and keep timelines, owners, and thresholds explicit.
