# Red Team Reference

Use this file when you need durable, high-level guidance for authorized red team leadership work inside the `red-team` skill.

This reference intentionally keeps to exercise management, capability framing, and reporting. It does not provide operational playbooks for intrusion, evasion, persistence, or social-engineering execution.

## Role definition

A red team leader directs simulated, goal-oriented security exercises to test an organization's overall security posture. The role combines offensive understanding with business judgment, communication, and team leadership.

The leader's job is not merely to "hack well." It is to design exercises that safely generate useful evidence about detection, response, resilience, and organizational blind spots.

## Strategic leadership skills

- Operational planning and scoping: define objectives, boundaries, rules of engagement, and acceptable risk.
- Risk management: make go or no-go decisions that protect production systems and business continuity.
- Stakeholder liaison: translate technical possibilities into business terms for executives, sponsors, legal teams, and security leadership.
- Team leadership and mentoring: coach junior practitioners, set quality standards, and watch for burnout.
- Decision discipline: document assumptions, approvals, escalations, and stop conditions.

## Technical fluency areas

A red team leader should understand these domains well enough to design credible exercises and evaluate outcomes:

- adversary-emulation frameworks and threat behavior mapping
- operating systems and identity environments
- networking fundamentals and segmentation realities
- cloud platforms and web application exposure
- defensive tooling such as SIEM, EDR, logging, and firewall controls
- scripting and tooling literacy for customization and automation

This fluency supports better planning and reporting. It does not require including exploit recipes in the skill.

## Collaboration and reporting

High-value red team work depends on communication quality:

- Produce an executive summary that explains business impact clearly.
- Produce technical findings that defenders can act on.
- Support purple-team collaboration when the goal is joint learning rather than surprise.
- Distinguish fact, inference, and recommendation.
- Show how multiple small weaknesses can combine into a larger failure path.

Treat the report as the real deliverable.

## Red team vs penetration testing

Use this distinction when the user needs comparison language:

- Red teaming is objective-driven and holistic. It tests people, process, and technology together.
- Penetration testing is usually narrower and vulnerability-centered. It often aims to identify as many weaknesses as possible within a defined target set.
- Red team exercises are typically judged by whether they validated assumptions about resilience, detection, or response, not by raw vulnerability count.

## Learning and development themes

When helping someone grow toward this role, focus on:

- networking and operating-system fundamentals
- scripting literacy
- defensive awareness
- exercise design
- writing and briefing skill
- mentorship and team management
- practice in lawful training environments

Frame certifications, labs, or platforms as optional ways to structure practice, not as substitutes for judgment.

## Planning prompts

Use these prompts when scoping or reviewing an exercise:

- What business question is this exercise trying to answer?
- What specific defender behaviors or controls are being tested?
- What is in scope, and what is explicitly out of scope?
- What would create unacceptable operational risk?
- Who can halt the exercise, and under what conditions?
- What evidence will be collected for both technical and executive reporting?
- What changes would make the result more actionable for defenders?
