# Publisher Role Map

Use this reference when the task depends on role boundaries, editorial and
publishing competencies, or the cover-and-package workflow for a book project.

## Core Editor Competencies

- Language and grammar control, including syntax, spelling, punctuation, and
  style-guide discipline.
- The editing "3 Cs": make the work clear, consistent, and correct.
- Developmental and structural editing: strengthen flow, pacing, tone,
  organization, and narrative coherence.
- Audience adaptation: shape voice and content for the intended readership and
  medium.
- Technical literacy with tools and formats often used in publishing, such as
  CMS workflows, SEO-aware copy practices, Microsoft Word, and Adobe InDesign.
- Constructive communication: give direct but usable feedback that helps the
  author revise without flattening the voice.

## Core Publisher Competencies

- Acquisitions and market research: evaluate a manuscript's commercial
  potential, genre fit, and timing.
- Project and financial management: coordinate authors, editors, designers,
  marketers, and schedules while watching budget and production risk.
- Networking and negotiation: work with authors, agents, distributors,
  retailers, and freelancers.
- Marketing strategy: position the title, shape discovery, and align the book
  with distribution channels.
- Metadata optimization: improve discoverability through title logic,
  categories, keywords, and packaging decisions.

## Shared Skills

- Time management under competing publication deadlines.
- Critical thinking about what is working, what is not, and why.
- Flexibility when project scope, audience, or market conditions shift.
- Cultural sensitivity and audience awareness.
- Clear collaboration across editorial, design, sales, and production.

## Decision Rights and Non-Goals

- Editors improve the manuscript and package language; they do not set pricing, print runs, or go/no-go publication decisions.
- Publishers decide positioning, budget, schedule, and release logic; they should not override editorial quality concerns without stating the tradeoff.
- Designers and art directors own visual execution and production readiness; they should not be asked to invent the market strategy alone.
- Shared deliverables such as jacket copy, metadata, and cover briefs need an explicit final approver so collaboration does not turn into silent drift.

## Cover and Package Role Split

| Deliverable | Primary Owner | What Good Ownership Looks Like |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Manuscript quality | Editor | Improve clarity, structure, pacing, tone, and adherence to style |
| Acquisitions decision | Publisher | Judge audience fit, trend alignment, cost, and likely return |
| Jacket copy and author bio | Editor | Write persuasive, accurate copy tied to the book's voice and promise |
| Cover concept brief | Editor + Publisher | Translate themes, audience, comps, and market signals into direction |
| Visual cover execution | Designer / Art Director | Build imagery, typography, layout, and genre signaling |
| Budget, finishes, pricing | Publisher | Approve spend on illustration, foil, embossing, or premium materials |
| Print-ready package | Designer + Production | Handle spine width, ISBN, barcode, bleeds, margins, and final files |
| Retail and channel fit | Publisher | Check thumbnail impact, bookstore appeal, metadata, and sell-through logic |

## Cover Workflow

1. Read the manuscript, synopsis, or key chapters for theme, tone, genre, and
   audience.
2. Build a cover memo or brief with the plot signal, central motifs, comp
   titles, target demographic, must-have cues, and avoid cues.
3. Draft the jacket copy and author bio so the verbal package matches the
   visual package.
4. Review cover concepts for alignment with tone, genre expectations, and
   retailer visibility.
5. Approve the final direction against budget, schedule, and production
   constraints.

## Designer-Specific Notes

- Designers own the execution layer: typography, imagery, layout, and
  print-prep details.
- Strong cover design depends on InDesign, Photoshop, Illustrator, and basic
  print-production knowledge.
- The designer translates the manuscript's themes into one legible, genre-aware
  sales image rather than a literal summary of the story.

## When Roles Blur

Use this quick rule:

- If the disagreement is about reader clarity or manuscript quality, the editor leads.
- If the disagreement is about audience, sales context, budget, or schedule, the publisher leads.
- If the disagreement is about execution quality within an approved brief, the designer leads.
- If the disagreement changes the core promise of the book, return to a shared positioning brief before moving forward.

## Useful Output Types

- Editorial letter
- Developmental revision memo
- Acquisitions memo
- Positioning brief
- Jacket copy and author bio
- Cover brief
- Metadata sheet
- Production handoff checklist
