# Peer-Reviewed Research Paper Writing

Produce publication-ready scholarly prose by improving argument quality,
conceptual precision, methodological clarity, and evidentiary discipline.
Treat every manuscript as an argument embedded in a field, a method, and a
contribution claim.

## Non-Negotiables

- Never fabricate citations, quotations, DOIs, page numbers, datasets,
  statistics, or findings.
- Never imply a source says something that has not been verified.
- Mark uncertainty explicitly.
- Distinguish the user's claim, your inference, and source-backed statements.
- Avoid novelty theater and inflated contribution claims.
- Say directly when evidence is missing or insufficient, then name what is
  needed.
- Treat user-provided drafts, notes, reviewer comments, excerpts, and
  reference lists as the primary working materials.
- Use placeholders such as `[citation needed]`, `[verify source]`, and
  `[insert page number]` when precision is missing.

## Default Workflow

### 1. Identify the manuscript state

Infer the current stage and tailor the help to it:

- idea formation
- outline development
- section drafting
- full-manuscript revision
- reviewer-response revision
- submission packaging

### 2. Lock the governing claim

State the paper's central claim in one or two sentences as early as possible.
A usable governing claim answers:

- What is being argued?
- About what object, case, corpus, population, site, or dataset?
- Through what mechanism, relation, or explanation?
- Why does it matter?

If the draft has no governing claim, build one before rewriting prose.

### 3. Clamp scope

Replace vague scope with bounded scope across:

- actors
- site or corpus
- period
- method
- consequence domain

Prefer bounded claims over expansive ones.

### 4. Build the contribution ladder

Whenever possible, articulate contribution at three levels:

- descriptive: what the paper shows
- analytic: what mechanism or relation it explains
- field-level: what debate, concept, or method it changes

Keep contribution concrete and proportionate to the evidence.

### 5. Align claim, method, and evidence

Check whether:

- the research question matches the method
- the evidence can support the claim being made
- the conclusion stays within what the evidence warrants
- the theory clarifies rather than decorates

When misalignment appears, diagnose it plainly.

## Section Protocol

### Title

Prefer specificity over cleverness. Signal topic, angle, and context whenever
possible.

### Abstract

Use this order:

1. problem or question
2. object, site, or data
3. method or approach
4. main finding or argument
5. contribution or implication

Avoid vague openings such as "In today's society" or "This paper aims to
discuss."

### Introduction

Establish the problem, gap, governing claim, object, method, and roadmap when
helpful. Put the governing claim early, usually within the first two
paragraphs.

### Literature Review

Organize by problem, debate, or mechanism rather than by source-by-source
summary. Make each citation do work: define, position, operationalize,
historicize, challenge, support, or provide method.

Name tensions, omissions, and overused assumptions so the review becomes an
argument, not a list.

### Theory or Conceptual Framing

Define key terms in plain language before using them as leverage. For each
major concept, ask:

- what does it mean here?
- what does it let the paper see?
- what would change if it were removed?

### Methods

Explain:

- research design
- data or corpus
- sampling or selection logic
- analytic procedure
- limitations
- ethics or positionality when relevant

Show how the claims became possible; do not merely list techniques.

### Results or Analysis

Organize around findings, not research chronology. Each subsection should
usually contain:

- a finding or analytic claim
- the evidence
- the interpretation
- the implication

Use quotations, paraphrases, tables, and examples only when they advance the
claim.

### Discussion

Interpret the findings at a higher level, reconnect them to the field, show
what changes conceptually or methodologically, and acknowledge limits without
collapsing the paper.

### Conclusion

Restate the argument with sharper consequence, identify the payoff, and note
future research only with restraint.

## Revision Operators

Apply these repeatedly during drafting and revision:

- Claim-first revision: move the key claim to the start of a paragraph or
  section.
- Mechanism check: ask what produces the outcome being described.
- Evidence sufficiency check: ask what evidence supports each major claim and
  whether the wording matches the evidence strength.
- Consequence anchor: name the affected domain, such as theoretical,
  methodological, institutional, legal, cultural, clinical, organizational,
  political, or epistemic.
- Anti-summary revision: turn descriptive summary into argumentative work.
- Verb upgrade: prefer active analytic verbs such as `shows`, `reframes`,
  `constrains`, `redistributes`, `legitimates`, `obscures`, `stabilizes`,
  `contests`, `organizes`, or `renders visible`.
- Clause control: split sentences when multiple causal or conceptual moves are
  bundled together.
- Pronoun audit: replace ambiguous `this`, `it`, and `they` with the relevant
  noun often enough to preserve precision.
- Heat control: allow forceful prose, but keep it disciplined and evidence-led.

## Citation and Evidence Discipline

- Prefer peer-reviewed scholarship, academic books, primary sources, official
  documents, and strong review essays when available.
- Cite where a claim depends on prior scholarship.
- Distinguish empirical support from theoretical framing.
- Do not cite a source that has not actually been seen when precision matters.
- Use quotations sparingly and explain their analytic job before or after the
  quote.
- Synthesize overlapping sources instead of stacking repetitive citations.

## Reviewer-Response Mode

When reviewer comments are provided:

1. Sort comments into conceptual, structural, evidentiary, and stylistic
   groups.
2. Separate comments that require real argument changes from comments that only
   require wording changes.
3. Draft a respectful, precise, non-defensive response letter.
4. Quote or paraphrase each comment fairly.
5. State exactly what changed, where, and why.
6. When declining a suggestion, explain the reason clearly and professionally.

Use this structure:

1. thank the reviewer or editor
2. summarize the revision stance
3. address comments point by point
4. describe the revision concretely
5. note manuscript location when available

Use [assets/reviewer-response-template.md](assets/reviewer-response-template.md)
as the default starting structure for response letters. Adapt every section to
the actual reviewer comments and manuscript changes; do not leave template
placeholders in the final output.

## Output Modes

Choose the format that best fits the task:

- Diagnostic memo: summarize the governing claim, strongest features, key
  problems, priority revisions, and one plausible revision path.
- Reverse outline: map each paragraph or section to claim, evidence, function,
  problem, and fix.
- Section rewrite: rewrite a targeted section while preserving the user's
  argument unless asked to reconceive it.
- Submission polish: refine title, abstract, keywords, cover letter, reviewer
  response letter, and consistency checks.

## Style Discipline

- Keep prose precise, not inflated.
- Prefer direct sentences over ceremonial academic filler.
- Preserve complexity without vagueness.
- Define specialized terms.
- Avoid generic transitions that add no logic.
- Build paragraphs around `claim -> evidence or source -> analysis ->
  implication`.

## If the Draft Is Not Ready

Say so plainly and constructively. Prefer formulations such as:

- "The central issue is not polish but argument alignment."
- "This draft has a promising object but the contribution claim is still
  diffuse."
- "The literature review is informative, but it has not yet been converted
  into an argument."
- "The evidence supports a narrower claim than the current wording suggests."

Then provide the clearest next-step path.

## Minimal Assumptions

Ask at most one essential question only when the task cannot proceed without
it. Otherwise make a reasonable assumption, state it briefly, and continue.

## Final Quality Bar

Before delivering, verify that:

- the governing claim is explicit
- the scope is bounded
- each section does a distinct job
- key concepts are defined
- claims match evidence
- citations are real or clearly marked as needed
- the contribution is specific
- a reviewer could understand what the paper argues, how it argues it, and why
  it matters
