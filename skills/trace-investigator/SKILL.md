# Trace Investigator

## Overview

Use this skill to trace how governance meaning travels across mixed documents instead of summarizing each document in isolation.

Prioritize authority flow, accountability handoffs, definition drift, exception paths, and monitoring narrowings. Always separate what is directly evidenced from what is inferred.

## Default workflow

### 1. Identify the documentary unit

State what is in scope as a document pack.

Capture visible provenance markers when present:
- document type
- date or revision
- owner or author
- medium or layer
- declared audience or purpose

Treat mixed media as a signal, not noise. A policy, checklist, appendix, email summary, and dashboard definition usually represent different layers where authority can shift.

### 2. Extract traces

Look for repeated wording, framing templates, and substitutions:
- repeated labels
- renamed thresholds
- softened mandatory language
- narrowed or widened categories
- passive voice around decision power
- summary language that changes tone without formal revision

Also extract:
- citation chains
- delegated crosswalks
- exception clauses
- monitoring definitions
- omissions that matter to enforcement

### 3. Map handoffs

Ask these questions explicitly:
- who defines key terms?
- who decides applicability?
- who sets thresholds or exceptions?
- who retains final decision power?
- who inherits authority from an upstream document?
- who disappears into collective nouns, system language, or passive voice?

Track where authority moves from formal gate language into softer channels such as:
- self-attestation
- rationale fields
- exception approval
- informal summaries
- dashboards
- tagging systems

### 4. Separate claim levels

Always sort claims into these levels:

- `Direct evidence`: plainly stated in the record
- `Inference`: supported by multiple traces across the pack
- `Speculation`: plausible but not grounded enough to assert

Do not blur these levels. If a strong conclusion depends on missing logs, missing crosswalks, or missing revision history, say so.

### 5. Describe how authority travels

Prefer a mechanism chain over a theme list.

Useful chains include:
- definitions -> procedures -> defaults -> dashboards
- policy -> template -> exception path -> summary -> monitoring
- threshold -> applicability -> approval -> release -> incident capture

Name where the pack becomes softer, more discretionary, or narrower than it first appears.

### 6. Write bounded conclusions

End with:
- the main trace
- supporting receipts
- strongest inference
- viable alternate reading
- what remains unproven
- next safe research steps

Keep the conclusion proportional to the packet. Do not claim intent, routine abuse, or actual practice unless the documents support that directly.

## Output shape

Use this structure unless the user wants another format:

### Documentary unit
- in-scope documents
- provenance markers

### Traces
- repeated wording or framing
- substitutions and narrowing/widening
- omissions and definitional gaps
- citation chains and summary laundering

### Handoffs
- who defines
- who decides
- who inherits
- who disappears

### Claim levels
- direct evidence
- inference
- speculation

### Authority travel
- short mechanism narrative

### Bounded conclusion
- main trace
- receipts
- alternate reading
- unproven points
- next research steps

## Decision rules

- Quote short phrases when wording itself is doing governance work.
- Prefer cross-document comparison over per-document summary.
- Treat templates, summaries, emails, and dashboards as places where authority often softens or narrows.
- Flag when a mandatory gate is structurally non-binding.
- Flag when monitoring definitions miss the harms the upstream policy claims to govern.
- Distinguish formal revision from informal norm change.
- When in doubt, choose exact over dramatic.

## Common patterns to watch

- mandatory language paired with advisory authority
- exception paths that move review from pre-launch to post-launch
- checklist applicability that converts controls into self-attestation
- summaries or emails that function like policy updates without change control
- dashboards that redefine harm through severity tags or support workflows
- crosswalk gaps between similar but non-identical classification regimes
