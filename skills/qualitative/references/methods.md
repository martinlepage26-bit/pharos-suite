# Qualitative Methods Map

Use this file when you need a compact comparison rather than a long survey.

## Route From Material First

- Interview transcripts -> thematic analysis, phenomenology, narrative inquiry, category-building analysis
- Focus groups -> thematic analysis, discourse analysis, interaction-focused analysis
- Fieldnotes and observation logs -> ethnography, participant observation, ethnomethodology
- Life stories and biographical interviews -> narrative inquiry, `recit de vie`, life-history work
- Life stories plus archives or witness accounts -> `histoire de vie`, ethnosociological analysis
- Policy, media, and institutional text -> discourse analysis, CDA
- Images, film, or photo-elicitation -> visual sociology, visual ethnography
- Digital communities, forum threads, or platform traces -> digital ethnography, discourse analysis
- Diaries, memoir, and reflective writing -> autoethnography, writing-mode analysis
- Sound, movement, smell, and embodied environment notes -> sensory ethnography

When material is thin, choose the least inferential method and state the limitation clearly.

## Core Designs

### Thematic Analysis

- Best for: identifying recurring patterns across a dataset
- Typical data: interviews, focus groups, open-ended surveys, fieldnotes
- Output: themes that answer a focused research question
- Analytic moves: familiarize, code, cluster, review, define, write
- Watch for: themes that merely restate topics instead of interpreting meaning

### Category-Based or Grounded-Theory-Like Analysis

- Best for: building concepts, dimensions, and relationships inductively
- Typical data: iterative interviews, observations, memos
- Output: conceptual categories or a process model
- Analytic moves: constant comparison, memoing, category refinement, theoretical sampling
- Watch for: stopping at description instead of abstraction

### Phenomenology

- Best for: describing lived experience and the felt structure of a phenomenon
- Typical data: in-depth interviews, reflective accounts
- Output: an account of the essence or core structure of experience
- Analytic moves: bracket assumptions, attend to meaning units, synthesize essence
- Watch for: drifting into biography or theme-counting

### Narrative Inquiry and Life-History Work

- Best for: temporality, identity, turning points, and trajectories
- Typical data: biographical interviews, diaries, archives
- Output: narrative arcs, plotlines, identity work, trajectory analysis
- Analytic moves: sequence events, identify turning points, compare stories
- Watch for: flattening sequence into static themes

French distinctions:

- `recit de vie`: a person's account of their own path
- `histoire de vie`: a fuller reconstruction using other materials such as archives or witness accounts

### Ethnography and Participant Observation

- Best for: culture, practice, norms, rituals, institutions, and everyday life
- Typical data: fieldnotes, observation, informal interviews, artifacts
- Output: a contextual account of how a world works from inside and outside
- Analytic moves: immerse, observe, participate, memo, compare emic and etic views
- Watch for: over-identifying with the field or missing hidden routines

### Discourse Analysis and Critical Discourse Analysis

- Best for: language, framing, ideology, and power
- Typical data: speeches, media, documents, transcripts, policy texts
- Output: interpretive account of how language constructs reality or hierarchy
- Analytic moves: examine wording, silences, categories, subject positions, power effects
- Watch for: ignoring context outside the text

### Ethnomethodology

- Best for: how people produce social order in real-time interaction
- Typical data: recordings of everyday talk, service encounters, institutional interactions
- Output: the practical methods people use to keep life intelligible
- Analytic moves: close interaction analysis, repairs, turn-taking, norm breaches
- Watch for: overgeneralizing from small interaction fragments

### Autoethnography and Writing-Mode Analysis

- Best for: connecting personal experience to broader culture or politics
- Typical data: journals, memory work, reflective writing
- Output: reflexive cultural critique grounded in situated experience
- Analytic moves: write, revisit, connect self to structure, interrogate positionality
- Watch for: confessional writing without analytic linkage

## Method-Selection Matrix

| Research goal | Best-fit method(s) | Key analytic move | Typical output |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Find patterns across cases | Thematic analysis | Code, cluster, define themes | Theme map with supporting excerpts |
| Build theory from data | Category-based or grounded-theory-like analysis | Constant comparison and category refinement | Conceptual framework or process model |
| Capture lived experience | Phenomenology | Identify meaning units and synthesize the felt structure | Account of the experience from the inside |
| Trace identity over time | Narrative inquiry or life-history work | Sequence events and turning points | Narrative arc or trajectory typology |
| Understand culture in context | Ethnography or participant observation | Thick description and cultural interpretation | Context-rich account of how a world works |
| Analyze language and power | Discourse analysis or CDA | Identify framing, categories, silences, and power effects | Critical interpretation of text or talk |
| Study everyday social order | Ethnomethodology | Analyze repairs, turn-taking, and local methods | Account of how order is produced in interaction |
| Researcher as analytic material | Autoethnography | Connect situated experience to broader structure | Reflexive narrative with cultural analysis |
| Analyze images, spaces, or platforms | Visual, sensory, spatial, or digital approaches | Multimodal comparison and interpretation | Visual or digital analytic account |

## Cross-Cutting Concepts

### Sampling and Saturation

- Use purposive sampling when selecting information-rich cases.
- Use theoretical sampling when categories are still emerging.
- Treat `saturation sociologique` as the point where new cases stop adding new structural insights to the social world under study.

### Emic and Etic

- `emic`: insider meanings and local categories
- `etic`: analyst categories used for comparison or explanation

Strong qualitative work often moves between both.

### Ethics and Reflexivity

- Document informed consent, especially in immersive or digital settings.
- Address power asymmetry and extractive research risks.
- State how the researcher's gender, race, class, language, profession, or history shapes access and interpretation.

### French terminology quick map

Use this when the user needs bilingual method notes or is moving between anglophone and francophone research traditions.

| English | French | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Thematic analysis | Analyse thematique | Often associated with Paille and Mucchielli in francophone teaching |
| Grounded theory | Theorie ancree / theorisation ancree | Terms vary by region and school |
| Participant observation | Observation participante | Standard ethnographic phrasing |
| Discourse analysis | Analyse de discours | May refer to several French-language traditions |
| Coding | Codage | `Arbre de codes` is a common term for code tree |
| Saturation | Saturation | `Saturation sociologique` is often used for Bertaux-style structural recurrence |
| Reflexivity | Reflexivite | Sometimes framed as `posture reflexive` |
| Thick description | Description dense | `Description epaisse` also appears in some contexts |

## Domain Variants

### Visual, Sensory, Spatial, and Digital Approaches

- Visual sociology: analyze images, film, photo-elicitation, and visual culture
- Sensory ethnography: foreground sound, smell, touch, movement, and atmosphere
- Queer or spatial ethnography: examine sexuality, gender, safety, surveillance, and space
- Digital ethnography or netnography: study online communities, platforms, and digital identity

### Critical and Intersectional Lenses

Use these when the question is explicitly about power, inequality, or marginalization:

- feminist epistemology
- Black feminist geographies
- postcolonial or decolonial studies
- environmental justice
- disability or crip studies
- queer of color critique
- critical race theory

These are not always stand-alone methods. Often they function as analytic lenses layered onto interviews, ethnography, narrative work, mapping, or discourse analysis.

## Quick Selection Prompts

- "What patterns recur across many accounts?" -> thematic analysis
- "What is this experience like from the inside?" -> phenomenology
- "How do people tell the story of a life or turning point?" -> narrative inquiry
- "How does this group actually live, work, or ritualize?" -> ethnography
- "How does language reproduce or resist power?" -> discourse analysis or CDA
- "How are concepts or social processes emerging from the data?" -> category-building analysis
- "How do online or image-based environments shape meaning?" -> digital or visual approaches
