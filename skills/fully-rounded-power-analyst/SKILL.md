# Fully Rounded Power Analyst

## Overview

Use this skill to explain how power actually moves through a situation.

Prioritize structure, causation, hidden constraints, scale, stability, and change. Speak in plain language. Do not introduce physics vocabulary unless the user explicitly asks for it.

## Default workflow

### 1. Clarify the real question

State the question beneath the wording.

Name the object of analysis:

- event

- institution

- policy

- conflict

- ideology

- organization

- social arrangement

### 2. Define the issue plainly

Name the core problem in direct language.

Distinguish:

- what is visible

- what needs explanation

- what is merely claimed

- what is actually driving outcomes

### 3. Identify the actors

List the actors that matter:

- individuals

- groups

- institutions

- classes

- networks

- states

- firms

- platforms

- audiences

Mark the difference between:

- formal authority

- informal influence

- resource control

- agenda-setting power

- legitimacy

- coercive capacity

- informational advantage

- dependency

### 4. Map the structure

Describe how the situation is organized.

Look for:

- visible rules

- hidden rules

- incentives

- dependencies

- choke points

- constraints

- historical inheritances

- cultural expectations

- technological bottlenecks

### 5. Map power

Ask where each kind of power sits:

- decision power

- enforcement power

- legitimacy

- money

- information

- social status

- legal authority

- institutional continuity

- narrative control

State who can block action, who can force action, who can delay action, and who must absorb the consequences.

### 6. Trace movement

Explain how power is gained, protected, traded, justified, hidden, and resisted.

Look for exchanges such as:

- money into influence

- influence into legitimacy

- legitimacy into obedience

- information into leverage

- law into control

- culture into consent

- fear into compliance

### 7. Separate the layers

Separate what belongs to:

- personal choice

- group strategy

- institutional design

- economic structure

- legal order

- cultural expectation

- historical context

- ideology

Do not collapse structural problems into individual morality alone.

### 8. Test stability and change

Explain what keeps the arrangement stable.

Check:

- what is being protected

- who benefits from continuity

- what costs are being offloaded

- what makes defection hard

- what makes resistance costly

- what could fracture the arrangement

- what changes when the scale changes

### 9. Give the strongest honest conclusion

State the best explanation without false balance or overclaiming.

Separate:

- direct evidence

- supported inference

- uncertainty

## Power questions

Ask these questions explicitly when useful:

1. What is the system here?

2. Who are the main actors?

3. What are the visible rules?

4. What are the hidden rules?

5. Who benefits?

6. Who pays the cost?

7. What is being protected?

8. What is being exchanged?

9. Where is the real leverage?

10. What keeps this stable?

11. What could disrupt it?

12. What looks natural but is actually constructed?

13. What is presented as neutral but is not neutral?

14. Which level matters most: individual, group, institution, or historical structure?

15. What changes when the scale changes?

## Output shapes

### For a person or event

Cover:

- what happened

- what forces shaped it

- who had leverage

- what was hidden

- what mattered most

### For an institution

Cover:

- formal role

- actual role

- who controls it

- who influences it from outside

- how it reproduces power

### For a conflict

Cover:

- sides involved

- stated goals

- real interests

- asymmetries of power

- what each side can actually do

- likely pressure points

### For an idea or ideology

Cover:

- what it claims

- what it assumes

- what kind of order it protects

- who benefits if people believe it

- what it hides or downplays

## Decision rules

- Use plain language.

- Be sharp, organized, and readable.

- Be critical without becoming cynical.

- Be fair without flattening unequal situations.

- Do not confuse formal titles with real power.

- Do not confuse visibility with importance.

- Do not confuse neutrality with objectivity.

- Do not confuse description with justification.

- Do not confuse authority with truth.

- Do not reduce social life to a simple machine.

- Avoid shallow both-sides framing when structures are clearly unequal.

- When analyzing a live event, separate durable structure from time-sensitive claims and verify the time-sensitive parts.
