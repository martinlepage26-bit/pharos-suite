# Redraft Rules

Use this file when rewriting a policy, rule, SOP, code of conduct, or training text.

## Preserve Hard Constraints

Before changing tone, isolate the parts that cannot be softened or implied:

- law or statutory wording
- physical safety requirements
- deadlines and thresholds
- audit trails or recordkeeping duties
- mandatory controls

Human-centered wording should reduce friction, not hide the obligation.

## Core Moves

- Use plain language over legal or technical jargon.
- Prefer verbs over abstract nouns.
- Use active voice so responsibility is visible.
- Say who acts, what they do, when they do it, and why it matters.
- Favor positive phrasing when it preserves clarity.
- Use short lists and short paragraphs.
- Replace broad abstractions with concrete actors or examples.

## Language Preferences

- Prefer `help` over `facilitate`.
- Prefer `use` over `utilize`.
- Prefer `apply` over `make an application`.
- Prefer `we` and `you` when partnership helps.
- Prefer `must` or `need to` for real obligations.
- Use `should` only for advisory guidance.
- Use `may` only for permission or discretion.

## Voice Choices

### Coach voice

Use for process rules and operational guidance.

- Example: `Submit the report by Friday so the team can close the week on time.`

### Teammate voice

Use for culture, ethics, and collaboration norms.

- Example: `We flag conflicts early so we can protect trust in the process.`

### Formal authority voice

Use when the rule is safety-critical, statutory, or enforcement-heavy.

- Example: `You must log every medication change before the end of the shift.`

## Editing Checklist

- Remove archaic terms such as `herein`, `aforementioned`, and `henceforth`.
- Remove redundant couplets such as `null and void` or `cease and desist` unless legally required.
- Put short conditions first and long exceptions last.
- Sequence the rule in the order a person experiences it.
- Add one sentence of purpose when the rule otherwise feels arbitrary.

## Before And After Pattern

Original:
`Employees shall ensure that all relevant documentation is submitted in a timely manner.`

Humanized:
`Send the required documents by Friday at 5 p.m. so payroll and compliance checks can finish on time.`

Why it works:

- It names the actor.
- It replaces vague timing with a real deadline.
- It explains the operational purpose.

## High-Stakes Example

Original:
`All personnel shall ensure that any transmission of personally identifiable information complies with the encryption standards in Appendix C of the Information Security Policy. Failure to comply may result in disciplinary action up to and including termination.`

Humanized:
`Encrypt personal data before sending it. Use the standards in Appendix C of the InfoSec Policy. If you are unsure whether the data counts as personal, check with your team lead before sending it. Non-compliance puts people's data at risk and may lead to disciplinary action.`

Why it works:

- It keeps the required action explicit.
- It gives a help path where the boundary may be unclear.
- It preserves the consequence without letting threat language dominate the rule.

## Failure Modes

- Sound warm but hide the obligation.
- Replace enforceable details with values language alone.
- Keep every sentence long because the source text is long.
- Use passive voice to avoid naming the responsible party.
